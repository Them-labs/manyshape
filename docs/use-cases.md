# Use cases & customers

The cooperative model (vendors ship a contract, nothing is scraped) decides who the customer is: the **app vendor**, not the end user. That shapes everything below.

## Who it's for

**Vertical B2B SaaS vendors.** The core customer. Any software whose sales cycle is dominated by "can you customize it for how we work?": EHR and health, legal practice management, logistics and TMS, field service, insurance claims, construction PM, property management. Today they staff a services or forward-deployed team to build per-customer variants, or they lose the deal. Manyshape replaces that labor. Each customer's users reshape the interface themselves, from one contract. This is the segment with hard-dollar ROI and existing budget, because they already pay for the customization work.

**Enterprise internal-tools and platform teams.** The people who build ops dashboards, admin panels, and CRUD apps, then drown in "add a column for team X" and "a different view for finance." Retool-adjacent, but per user instead of per app. They adopt Manyshape to stop being the bottleneck.

**Malleable-first startups.** New apps that adopt the contract on day one and market "adapts to you" as a feature. Nothing to give up, everything to gain. The easiest early logos, because adoption is frictionless.

**AI app and agent-platform builders.** Products that already generate UI and need it to be safe, persistent, and updatable. They buy Manyshape as infrastructure (the sandbox, contract, and activation gate), not as an end-user tool.

**Prosumers and power users.** The consumer Personal tier. Not where revenue concentrates, but the demand signal that pulls vendors in.

## What they build

- **Same app, different personas.** A CRM where an SDR wants a call queue, an AE wants a pipeline board, and a manager wants a forecast dashboard. One contract, three products, zero extra vendor work.
- **Role-based views in vertical software.** Nurse, doctor, and billing each reshape intake and charting in an EHR without the vendor shipping three UIs.
- **Services replacement.** Enterprise SaaS sells "customizable by your own people's agents" instead of a six-figure integration engagement.
- **Power-user ergonomics.** Traders, support agents, and recruiters who live in one tool eight hours a day and want it dense and keyboard-first.
- **Accessibility as a first-class variant.** High-contrast, simplified, dyslexia-friendly, and screen-reader-optimized surfaces from one contract. Otherwise a huge cost.
- **Team interface packs.** An admin builds "our intake flow" once and pushes it to 200 seats. This is where the create-once, apply-everywhere dynamic monetizes (the Team tier).
- **Cross-app workflows.** "Invoice email to line items to accounting entry," composed by the user's agent within declared capabilities.

## The beachhead

Start with **vertical B2B SaaS that has painful per-customer customization**. It is the one segment where all three are true:

1. They already pay for the labor Manyshape replaces, so there is budget and ROI.
2. They own their own app, so adopting the contract is their decision, with no third-party permission needed.
3. The cooperative model fits perfectly.

Land three to five of those as design partners before chasing horizontal apps.

## Two things to be clear-eyed about

- **The customer is the vendor, and adoption is the whole risk.** You cannot reshape apps that have not opted in, so growth is gated on vendors shipping contracts. The consumer and extension side creates demand pressure on vendors, but it is not the revenue engine.
- **Most end users will not customize.** Value concentrates in admins and power users who create shared setups for everyone else. Design and price for that (Team and packs), not for the median user tweaking their own screen.
